Ghar ka khana vs restaurant food: an honest comparison

6 min read · by Kitchen by Iqra, a home kitchen in Jubilee Town

Quick answer: for the daily 1 PM meal, homemade food wins on oil, cost and how you feel at 3 PM; restaurants win for occasions and equipment-heavy dishes. If you eat lunch out 20+ times a month in Lahore, switching to a home kitchen typically saves PKR 6,000–10,000 per person — and your afternoons.

The oil test

Tilt a restaurant karahi and watch the red layer slide. That layer isn't carelessness — it's strategy. Commercial food must look rich in photos, taste loud on the first bite, and survive hours on a warmer, and oil does all three jobs. Home cooking has the opposite incentive: the cook eats from the same pot. At our kitchen the masala is bhuna properly and then the oil is measured the way Iqra measures it for her own family, because it literally is the same handi. The difference is invisible on Instagram and unmistakable in your stomach.

The freshness math

A restaurant prepping forty dishes can't cook each one to order; bases are made in bulk and revived through the day. A home kitchen runs the other direction: a short daily menu, cooked once, finished. That's why home kitchens close same-day orders around 11 AM — there's no second batch, and that limitation is exactly the quality guarantee. Yesterday's nihari doesn't exist here because yesterday's nihari was eaten yesterday.

The money, over a month

One lunch is a rounding error; a month is a budget line. A homemade plate from our menu costs PKR 300–600. A comparable restaurant lunch with delivery in Lahore now lands around PKR 700–1,000 once fees join the party. Over 22 working days, that's roughly PKR 8,800–13,200 for home food against PKR 15,000–22,000 for restaurant food — per person. For a ten-person office, the yearly gap funds an actual annual trip, which is a strange thing for daal to be capable of, but here we are.

Restaurant food is a guest. Ghar ka khana is family. You don't want guests every single day.

The 3 PM slump is a menu choice

Heavy oil, extra salt and sweetened sauces spike you and then drop you — the famous post-lunch coma is mostly a recipe decision someone else made for you. Lighter home masala, fresh roti and a daal-sabzi rotation keep the afternoon usable. Offices that switch tell us the same thing in different words: meetings after lunch stopped being a punishment.

Where restaurants honestly win

Fairness matters: a home kitchen is not the answer to everything. Live tandoor naan, charcoal BBQ, Lahori fried fish, elaborate desserts — these need equipment and crowds that homes don't have. Celebrations deserve a table and a waiter. The mistake isn't loving restaurants; it's outsourcing all 22 monthly lunches to a model built for occasions. Keep restaurants for the nights that matter, and give the daily 1 PM slot back to ghar ka khana.

Switching without drama

Try it as an experiment, not a commitment: order homemade for one week — our weekly planner makes that a two-minute job for a whole office — and watch three things: how the food tastes, what the week cost, and what 3 PM felt like. The daal will make the rest of the argument.

Frequently asked questions

Is homemade food really cheaper for daily lunch?
Usually, yes — PKR 300–600 per homemade plate against PKR 700–1,000 for a typical restaurant delivery lunch, which compounds to several thousand rupees per person every month.
Why does restaurant food feel heavier?
More oil, salt and colour, plus reheating — all rational choices for a commercial kitchen, none of them kind to your afternoon.
When should I still pick a restaurant?
Occasions, variety nights, and equipment-heavy dishes like live tandoor breads and charcoal BBQ. Daily lunch is where home kitchens shine.
